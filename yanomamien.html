Yanomami

The Yanomami are an indigenous group that inhabits the Amazon rainforest region, covering parts of Brazil and Venezuela. Its total population is estimated at around 35,000 to 40,000 individuals, making it the largest isolated indigenous population in the Americas. The Yanomami are known for their language, which is from the Yanomami language family, and for their rich culture, which includes subsistence practices based on hunting, fishing and slash-and-burn agriculture, as well as complex kinship systems and shamanic rituals. However, they face significant challenges, including conflicts with miners, loss of territory, and threats to their health from diseases introduced by outsiders.

The work of shamans

Once initiated, the Yanomami shamans can call the xapiripë to them, so that they act as auxiliary spirits. This power of knowledge/vision and communication with the world of “vital images/essences” (utupë) makes shamans the pillars of Yanomami society. A shield against the evil powers originating from humans and non-humans that threaten the lives of members of their communities, they are also tireless negotiators and warriors of the invisible, dedicated to taming the entities and forces that move the cosmological order.

They control the fury of thunder and storm winds, the regularity of the alternation of day and night, drought and rain, the abundance of game, the fertility of crops, they support the vault of the sky to prevent its fall (the current earth is an ancient fallen sky), ward off supernatural predators from the forest, counterattack the attacks of aggressive spirits from enemy shamans and, most importantly, cure the sick, victims of human malevolence (sorcery, aggressive shamanism, attacks on the animal double) or not -human (coming from evil beings në waripë).

The xapiripë spirits

The initiation of shamans is painful and ecstatic. Throughout it, inhaling the hallucinogenic yãkõana powder for many days (resin or fragments of the inner bark of the Virola sp. tree, dried and pulverized) under the guidance of the elders, they learn to "see/know" the xapiripë spirits and to "respond" to its corners. The xapiripë are seen in the form of miniature humanoids adorned with colorful and bright ceremonial ornaments. Their presentation dance is compared to the noisy and joyful arrival of invited groups, richly adorned, at an intercommunal reahu festival. They are, above all, shamanic "images" (utupë) of forest beings.

Yãkõana hallucinogenic powder inhalation session/Maloca Toototobi. Photo: Milton Guran, 1991.

There are xapiripë of mammals, birds, fish, batrachians, reptiles, lizards, chelonians, crustaceans and insects. There are spirits of different trees, spirits of leaves, spirits of vines, wild honey, water, stones, waterfalls... Many are also "images" of cosmic entities (moon, sun, storm, thunder, lightning) and characters mythological. There are also humble homemade xapiripë, such as the spirit of the dog, the spirit of fire or the clay pot. There are, finally, spirits of "white people" (the napënapëripë, mobilized, through symbolic homeopathy, to combat epidemics) and their domestic animals (chicken, ox, horse).

See the xapiripë spirits

To carry out their sessions, shamans inhale yãkõana powder, considered to be the food of the spirits. Under its effect, they say they "die": they enter a state of visionary trance during which they "call" themselves and "bring down" several auxiliary spirits, with whom they end up identifying themselves, imitating the choreographies and songs of each one in function. of their mobilization in pajelança (shamans are called xapiri thëpë, "spirit people"; doing pajelança is called xapirimu, "acting as a spirit"). Thus, when "their eyes die", the shamans acquire a vision/power that, unlike the illusory perception of "common people" (kua përa thëpë), gives them access to the essence of phenomena and the time of their origins, therefore, the ability to modify its course.
