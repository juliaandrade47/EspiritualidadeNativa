Kaingang

The Kaingang are a people belonging to the Jê family who currently occupy 46 indigenous lands located in States of São Paulo, Paraná, Santa Catarina and Rio Grande do Sul and their population is estimated at 37,470 people As they are distributed across four states, the situation of the communities presents the most varied conditions. In all cases, however, its social structure and cosmological principles remain in force, always updated by the different circumstances they have been going through.

Source: Gov.br Ministry of Indigenous Peoples,

Cosmolology and mythology

The dispersion of Kaingang groups across the fields and forests of their traditional territory did not and does not prevent that these indigenous people recognize a common cosmological system. Indeed, even today kaingang groups, in addition to a record common mythology, they share beliefs and practices regarding their ritual experiences – deep respect for the dead and attachment to the lands where their navels are buried indisputable expressions of the structuring value of cosmology for these Indians.

Origin Myth

Few studies are dedicated exclusively to the analysis of kaingang myths. There are, however, references recurring to myths collected by Borba (1882), Nimuendajú (1913) and Schaden (1956). We owe the first record of Kaingang mythology to Telêmaco Borba, who published, in 1882, the myth of the people's origin kaingang and the myth of the origin of corn. The first tells the story of the mythological brothers Kamé and Kairu who, after the great flood, left the interior of the Crinjijimbé mountain range. “In times gone by, there was a great flood that submerged the entire land inhabited by our ancestors. Only the summit of the Crinjijimbé mountain range emerged from the waters..” ,

Vicente Fokãe, leader of PI Xapecó, at Kikikoi (ritual of the Dead). Photo: Kimiye Tommasino, 2000.

After the waters dried up, the Caingangues settled in the vicinity of Crinjijimbé. The Cayrucrés and Camés, whose souls had gone to live in the center from the mountains, they started to make his way inside her; after a lot of work they ended up exiting through two paths” (Borba 1908:20-21).

The division into halves of Kamé and Kairu, the red thread to which Nimuendajú refers, appears in the origin myth through the trajectory of the mythological brothers Kamé and Kairu. It is these cultural heroes who give the kaingang moieties their name, they are the ones who, in the course of the myth, created the beings of nature.

“Kanyerú made snakes, Kamé, jaguars. He first made a jaguar and painted it, then Kanyerú made a deer. Kamé said to the jaguar: ‘Eat the deer, but don’t eat us’. Then he made a tapir, ordering it to eat people and animals. The tapir, however, did not understand the order. Kamé repeated it twice in vain; then he said to him, angrily: ‘You’re going to eat nettle leaves, you’re no good!’. Kanyeru made snakes and ordered them to bite men and animals” (Nimuendajú 1986:87).

The dualism expressed in the kaingang origin myth, as analyzed in light of Nimuendajú's contribution, presents two fundamental classificatory properties. Firstly, Kamé and Kairu dualism offers a comprehensive, totalizing system of classification – beings in nature, including men, bear the mark of halves and carry values ​​associated with them, such as: strong/weak, high/low, momentum/persistence. Secondly, kaingang dualism, in its mythological record, offers a formula for social organization through the establishment of rules of descent and marriage.

Marriage and descendants

The mythological brothers Kamé and Kairu not only created the beings of nature, but also the rules of conduct for men, defining the formula for recruiting the moieties (patrilineality) and establishing the way in which the halves should to relate (exogamy). “They arrived at a large field, met with the Kaingang and decided to marry the young men and women. The Kairucrés first married the daughters of the Kamés, the latter married those of the former, and as there were still men left, they married the daughters of the Kaingang” (Borba 1908:22).

According to kaingang tradition, marriages must be performed between individuals of opposite halves; the Kamé must marry the Kairu and vice versa. If marriages, ideally, unite members of the Kamé and Kairu moieties. The children of this ideal marriage receive filiation from their paternal half. Several ethnological records reveal the occurrence of patrilineal descent.

Teschauer (1927), for example, states that according to the kaingang tradition “the child owed his existence exclusively to the father. The mother was only the custodian and guardian of the offspring (...) the father's status was passed on to the children and not the mother's” (Teschauer 1927:44). Despite the patrilineal pattern of descent, the traditional form of residence among the Kaingang is matrilocality - after marriage, the son-in-law lives in his father-in-law's house. This pattern of residence is common to all Gê groups. As we have seen, belonging to a moiety comes from paternal descent. The ratification of this identity occurs with the choice of a name for the newborn.

Naming among the Jê, and specifically among the Kaingang, has been described as an important process for the establishment of social identities. The mythological heroes Kamé and Kairu themselves created and named the beings of nature. The names belong to the halves. Children, when named, receive their social identity, which, alongside their paternal descent, will be their distinguishing mark. Unlike descent, which cannot be changed, names can be manipulated in order to protect the child against illness or other misfortune.
