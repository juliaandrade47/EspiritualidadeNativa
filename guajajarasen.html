Guajajaras

The Guajajara are an indigenous group that mainly inhabits the state of Maranhão, in northeastern Brazil. Their total population is estimated at around 25,000 to 30,000 people, making them one of the largest indigenous ethnicities in the region. Gods

The Guajajara are a tribe with several gods and heroes, which are:

  • Maíra and the twins Maíra-ira and Mucura-ira: They are responsible for creating and shaping the world, being the most important.
  • Zuripari: It is responsible for the creation of pests, venomous snakes, pests and insects.
  • the "owners" of the forests (Ka'a Zar), the waters (Y Zar), the pants (Miar'i'zar) and the trees (Wira'zar).
  • We have the azang as mythological creatures, wandering spirits of the dead, also very feared.

Beliefs

Cosmology, mythology and rites. Traditional cosmology is typical of the Tupi-Guarani people, distinguishing four categories of supernatural beings, which receive the generic designation of karowara: the creators or cultural heroes, responsible for the creation and transformation of the world, being Maíra and the twins Maíra-ira and Mucura-ira the most important and Zurupari, the creator of pests and insects, venomous snakes and spiders, a much feared cultural hero; the "owners" of the forests (Ka'a'zar), the waters (Y'zar), the hunts (Miar'i'zar) and the trees (Wira'zar), who are hostile and greatly feared for their evil power ; the azang, wandering spirits of the dead, also greatly feared; and the piwara, animal spirits. Many Guajajara no longer believe in these beings, because of missionary activities.

Legends

The mythical motif of twins is common among several Tupi peoples. For the Guajajara they are cultural heroes, alongside Maíra-pai, although they do not have the same father. While Maíra-ira has divine origin, Mucura-ira has animal origin, like his father. The myth tells of his odyssey through a world full of challenges and dangers, from the first moments inside his mother's womb to his final meeting with Maíra. The biggest challenge is their survival among the "jaguars", who are cannibals and kill their mother, but the twins take brutal revenge on them. Over the years, the two learn to overcome all natural and supernatural dangers, but Mucura-ira suffers more due to her "human" nature. The myth is full of allusions to the daily life of the Guajajara and explains a large part of their world, such as, for example, the "condemnation" of the Guajajara to agriculture by Maíra because of some "original sin", as a moment of distrust of the powers of Maíra by a woman. But it can also be interpreted in terms of conflicts presented and overcome as a mythical representation of conflicts within Guajajara society and with other peoples.

Rituals

The great traditional rituals have been in decline for a long time. In the past, the most important was the Honey Festival (zemuishi-ohaw), held in September or October, during the dry season, and which required several months to prepare. It played a very important role in good relations between villages, but nowadays it is celebrated rarely and only in a few villages. The Corn Festival (awashire-wehuhau), also called the "shaman festival", took place every year during the rainy season, during the growing season of this vegetable. Its purpose was to ensure a good harvest and protect the corn against the actions of the Azang. Therefore, his main characteristic was pajelança. The Moqueado rite, held on the same occasion as part of the Corn Festival, marked the end of puberty for the participating teenagers. Moqueado is still practiced at irregular intervals, but it has become somewhat profane, often leaving only the culinary part of the rite to accompany political meetings.
