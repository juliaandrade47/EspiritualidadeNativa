Pataxó

Pataxó is a language from the Macro-Jê trunk and the Maxakalí linguistic family. The Pataxó Indigenous people are practitioners of shamanism and Christianity. In 2010, 11,436 inhabitants (5,839 men and 5,597 women) were registered by SIASI (Indigenous Health Care Information System) and FUNASA (National Health Foundation) distributed in several villages, in the extreme south of the State of Bahia in 36 villages distributed across six Indigenous Lands, and in the state of Minas Gerais the Pataxó live in seven communities.
Aldeia Velha, tribe of Pataxós Indians in Arraial D’Ajuda, district of Porto Seguro, Bahia.

Ritual practices and festivals

Ritual do Awê

The Awê ritual is the only one considered “an ancient thing”. It is “something that has always existed and that not even grandparents Some of the old people knew how to say when it started [...]. It seems that when an Awê was made in the past it was a single music/dance all the time. But doing an Awê is an expression that today refers to different party contexts [...] it encompasses a very varied set of choreographies, each with a specific meaning” (Grunewald, 1999, p. 251). Awê requires cauim and, eventually, aluá, a fermented drink made from ground corn grains or fruit peels, such as pineapple, among others.

Awê ritual held at the Indigenous Shopping Center in Aldeia de Coroa Vermelha. Photo: Sarah Siqueira de Miranda, 2006.

On the other hand, the existence of Toré, among the Pataxó, has always been denied: “Toré is from the north, not ours”. Some, like shaman Manoel Santana, react very negatively, to the possibility of admitting this practice, under the argument that “you cannot copy that, no, it is not wow, when the people from the north arrive, we'll be embarrassed and won't be able to sing that there. Each represents what is yours. Represent what belongs to others?”

Indigenous rituals, in the ethnographic context of the Northeast, have a strong emphasis on their private character, under the form of the “secret”. It is common, therefore, to refer to a Toré that can be shared with assistants who are not indigenous people, and another, private, whose participation constitutes an exclusive prerogative of the indigenous people. The Awê of the Pataxó established in the extreme south of Bahia seems to emphasize public expression, contrary to what has observed in relation to Toré, considered by them as being “from the Indians of the Northeast, further up”. Sandro Campos Neves observes, however, that Awê in Coroa Vermelha is presented both in the public context and in intimate and exclusive contexts, such as land reclamation celebrations or celebrations in the Reserva da Jaqueira (Neves, 2012, p. 155).

According to Grunewald's description, leader Nelson Saracura believes that the Coroa Vermelha Indians are “rescuing an ancestral ceremony”, but that this rescue cannot be shown to non-indigenous people, “because There has to be a secret to the ritual, the secret is security, a secret is the resistance of us as an indigenous area”. Thus, Saracura states that the Coroa Vermelha community has intended to represent the “two parties”, that is, both Awê (from Barra Velha) and Toré from the Paraguassu-Caramuru Indigenous Reserve, where Among other ethnicities, the Pataxó HãHãHãe, or northern Pataxó, reside (Grunewald, 2008, p. 261).

Arsgwaksá

In August, Arsgwaksá is celebrated annually, the celebration of the anniversary of the Jaqueira Project, when, simultaneously, Pataxó culture is conveyed. The festivities include presentation of Awê, physical tests such as log races and different modalities of “public representation of Pataxó Indianness”, such as the traditional weddings, preceded by a demonstration of physical strength by the male suitors, the transport of wooden logs, the same ones used in competitions in indigenous games (Neves, 20123, p. 166-167).

The so-called Holy Week was referred to as an occasion for the use of gourd masks, each under a different denomination. “There was a guy called Mandu, an animal with a big head, caipora, ox... He came out during Holy Week”. It is, even today, a period during which more formal habits are manifested, such as treating the elderly with a certain reverence, such as taking their blessings, kneeling, as an expression of the effective or presumptive kinship that unites the young to the oldest: “Blessings, my uncle! My cousin! My godfather!". Apparently, there is no direct and conscious connection, for the Pataxó, between Holy Week – a Christian festival commemorating the resurrection of Christ – and the use of animal masks. There would, most likely, only be the memory of a temporal marker of Christian influence.
