Kayapó

The Kayapó, also known as Kaiapó or Kajkai, are an indigenous group that inhabits the region of Amazon, mainly in the states of Pará and Mato Grosso, in Brazil. Its total population is estimated at around 8,600 to 9,000 individuals, distributed across several villages in a vast area of ​​tropical forest. The Kayapó speak several languages ​​belonging to the Jê linguistic family, with regional variations. They are known for its rich culture and traditions, including crafts, shamanic rituals and subsistence practices based in hunting, fishing and agriculture.

Cosmology and rituals

The village is the center of the Kayapó universe, the most socialized space. The surrounding forest is considered as an anti-social space, where men can transform into animals or spirits, fall ill for no reason or even kill your relatives; There live beings that are half-animals, half-people. The further from the village, the more antisocial becomes the forest and more dangers are associated with it. As there is always the danger that "social" is appropriate through the domination of nature, escaping human control, the Kayapó seek a symbolic appropriation of the natural, turning it into social through healing songs and ceremonies that establish a constant exchange between man and the world of nature. The portion of the forest in which the village population hunts, fishes and plows is socialized by assigning place names . Then, human interventions in nature are accompanied by rituals. For example, the Installation of new fields is preceded by a dance that has many structural similarities with the war ritual.

Rituals

Kayapó rituals are numerous and diverse, but their importance and duration vary greatly. They are divided into three main categories: the large ceremonies confirming personal names; certain agricultural, hunting, fishing and seasonal rites - for example, those performed during a solar or lunar eclipse - and, finally, rites of passage. The latter are often extremely solemn, but short, and rarely accompanied by dancing or singing: they are organized to publicly announce the passage of some people from one age class to another. Examples of rites of passage, all ceremonies are qualified by the term mereremex ("people who extend their beauty"), which refers to the currently elaborate way in which men adorn themselves on such occasions. Such ceremonies constitute group activities whose purpose is to socialize "savage" or antisocial values. It is like this in relation to the attribution of the name, a central theme of most Kayapó ceremonies; in effect, personal names are borrowed from nature. It is the shamans who come into contact with natural spirits and learn new songs and names from them. These names, along with the songs to which they refer, are elements borrowed from the "natural" world, and must therefore be introduced into the culture at the time of great ceremonies.

The living and the dead

The Kayapó think that the spirits of the dead live in a separate village, somewhere in the hills. This is organized like that of the living: in the form of a circle with one or two men's houses, they have male and female associations, age classes, etc. The essential difference lies in the fact that spirits live at night and fear the light of day. This is why the Kayapó are afraid of being alone in the forest at night. The women smoke during almost their entire stay in the fields, as the spirits fear the smoke. Without this precaution, many spirits would stalk them when they went to collect potatoes and cassava and would then follow them to the village. To confuse the spirits' plan, the women spit in all directions before leaving the fields and surround themselves with a cloud of smoke. Spitting and blowing smoke are acts with the same effectiveness as male chants after a successful hunt: both aim to scare away the spirits.
