Ticunas

The Ticuna people, also known as Tikuna, Tukuna or Magüta, are indigenous inhabitants of the Amazon Trapezium, residing on the borders of Peru and Brazil, as well as in Colombia. With a population of over 50,000 individuals, They are the most populous indigenous people in the Amazon region of Brazil, with a demographic division of 36,000 individuals in Brazil, 8,000 in Colombia and 7,000 in Peru. The Ticuna language is often considered an isolated language, but there is possibility of being related to the Yuri language, now extinct.

The Ticuna (also Magüta, Tucuna, Tikuna or Tukuna) are indigenous peoples of Brazil, Colombia and Peru.

Gods and Beliefs

The Ticunas believe in an ancient story that describes a period of darkness on Earth due to an immense samaum tree, called a wotchine, which blocked the light. The gods Yo'i and Ipi threw fruit pits into the tree, creating stars when the sloth at the top did not allow the light to pass through. After failed attempts by animals to fell the tree, the gods offered sister Aicüna in marriage to whoever could throw fire ants into the royal sloth's eyes. Taine, a small rodent, accepted the challenge, blinded the sloth, and the tree finally fell, bringing the light.

Ticuna mythology also highlights reverence for nature. One example is the myth of the ngewane, an enchanted tree that, after the windy and rainy season, produces eggs that transform into different species of fish. This tree is seen as a guarantee of abundance of food. Another sacred tree, the tüerüma, has branches that, when they fall, transform into jaguars (through the leaves on the right) and hawks (through the leaves on the left). These legends emphasize the Ticuna's intimate connection with the forest and its natural elements.
